The Myth of Alignment

An Operational Reflection on Coordination and Intent

An Operational Reflection on Coordination and Intent

One of the most common phrases in organizations is:

"We are aligned."

The statement is often made with confidence.

Sometimes with pride.

Occasionally with relief.

Unfortunately, it is frequently untrue.

What many organizations call alignment is actually:

  • Agreement
  • Compliance
  • Awareness
  • Attendance

True alignment is something else entirely.

What Alignment Actually Means

Alignment exists when independent people, teams, and organizations make decisions that consistently support a common objective.

Not because they were told to.

Because they understand the objective and their role within it.

The distinction matters.

Agreement is easy.

Alignment is difficult.

People can agree during a meeting and pursue entirely different goals afterward.

People can acknowledge a strategy and interpret it differently.

People can support the same mission while prioritizing conflicting outcomes.

On paper, everyone appears aligned.

In reality, the system behaves otherwise.

Alignment Exists at Multiple Levels

This happens because alignment operates at multiple levels.

  • Alignment of goals
  • Alignment of incentives
  • Alignment of authority
  • Alignment of priorities
  • Alignment of information
  • Alignment of understanding

Failure at any one of these levels can produce divergence.

Even when everyone genuinely wants the same outcome.

The Optimization Problem

Consider a common organizational scenario.

Leadership prioritizes security.

Operations prioritizes availability.

Developers prioritize delivery.

Compliance prioritizes audit readiness.

Finance prioritizes cost control.

Each objective is reasonable.

Each objective may even support the broader mission.

Yet conflict emerges.

Not because anyone is wrong.

Because the system lacks alignment.

Each group is optimizing according to a different definition of success.

The result is predictable.

  • Meetings increase.
  • Coordination increases.
  • Friction increases.
  • Progress slows.

Observers often conclude that communication is the problem.

Communication may help.

But communication alone does not create alignment.

People can communicate constantly while remaining fundamentally misaligned.

The challenge runs deeper.

Alignment requires shared understanding.

Shared understanding requires context.

Context requires discovery.

Why Alignment Drifts

This is why alignment is difficult to achieve and easy to lose.

A single change in incentives can undermine it.

A change in leadership can undermine it.

A change in priorities can undermine it.

A change in resources can undermine it.

The system drifts.

Often gradually.

Sometimes invisibly.

Eventually, different parts of the organization begin operating according to different assumptions.

The divergence grows.

The organization continues claiming alignment.

Reality disagrees.

Complexity Makes Alignment Harder

The problem becomes more severe in large systems.

The larger the organization, the more interpretations emerge.

The more interpretations emerge, the greater the likelihood of divergence.

This is not a failure of intelligence.

It is a consequence of complexity.

Different people experience different realities.

Different teams operate under different constraints.

Different leaders receive different information.

Without continuous effort, alignment naturally degrades.

The Illusion of Alignment

Many organizations respond by creating:

  • Additional governance
  • Additional reporting
  • Additional meetings
  • Additional controls

These actions may help.

But they can also create the illusion of alignment.

The appearance of alignment is not the same thing as alignment itself.

A room full of people nodding in agreement may simply indicate that nobody wishes to argue.

A signed strategy document may simply indicate that everyone signed the document.

A completed briefing may simply indicate that information was presented.

None of these guarantee shared understanding.

And without shared understanding, alignment remains fragile.

The Better Question

The most effective leaders recognize this.

They spend less time asking:

"Did everyone receive the message?"

And more time asking:

"Do people understand the objective the same way?"

The difference is profound.

One measures communication.

The other measures alignment.

Alignment is not achieved when people hear the same words.

Alignment is achieved when people make compatible decisions in the absence of direct supervision.

The Real Test

That is the real test.

What happens when nobody is watching?

What happens when priorities conflict?

What happens when tradeoffs become necessary?

What happens when conditions change?

The answers reveal whether alignment truly exists.

Because alignment is not a meeting.

It is not a briefing.

It is not a slide deck.

It is not a signature.

Alignment is visible in behavior.

And behavior is far more difficult to fake.

Organizations often believe alignment exists because everyone agreed.

Systems reveal alignment through what everyone actually does.

The difference between those two realities explains many organizational failures.

Final Thought

Alignment is powerful.

But alignment is also rare.

Much rarer than organizations like to believe.

Which is why alignment should never be assumed.

It should be continuously verified.

Just like trust.
