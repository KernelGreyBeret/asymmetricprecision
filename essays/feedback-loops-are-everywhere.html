An Operational Reflection on Cause and Effect

An Operational Reflection on Cause and Effect

Most people think systems behave in straight lines.

A cause produces an effect.

An action produces a result.

A decision produces an outcome.

Reality is rarely that simple.

Most complex systems operate through feedback loops.

The output of the system becomes an input to the system.

The effect influences the cause.

The system begins reacting to itself.

This changes everything.

What appears to be a simple event is often part of a cycle.

The cycle repeats.

The cycle amplifies.

The cycle suppresses.

The cycle evolves.

Over time, the loop becomes more important than the individual event.

The System Reacts to Itself

This is why complex systems often behave in ways that seem surprising.

People focus on isolated actions.

The system is responding to accumulated feedback.

Consider trust.

A leader trusts their team.

The team receives greater autonomy.

Performance improves.

Trust increases further.

Additional autonomy follows.

The cycle reinforces itself.

Trust creates behavior.

Behavior creates trust.

The loop becomes self-sustaining.

The opposite can happen as well.

Leadership loses trust.

Oversight increases.

Decision-making slows.

Performance declines.

Leadership loses additional trust.

More oversight follows.

The system enters a downward spiral.

The same people may be involved.

The same mission may exist.

The feedback loop changed.

The outcome changed with it.

Reinforcing and Balancing Loops

Organizations are full of these cycles.

Success attracts resources.

Resources create capability.

Capability produces additional success.

The organization grows.

Growth increases complexity.

Complexity slows decisions.

Slower decisions reduce effectiveness.

Effectiveness declines.

Resources become constrained.

The same success that enabled growth may eventually create its own challenges.

The system is reacting to itself.

The loop continues.

Markets behave this way.

Governments behave this way.

Relationships behave this way.

Technology behaves this way.

Cybersecurity behaves this way.

When Interventions Create New Problems

A security incident occurs.

Controls are added.

Complexity increases.

Users find workarounds.

Risk increases.

Additional controls are added.

The cycle continues.

Each intervention changes the environment.

The changed environment produces new behavior.

The new behavior creates new outcomes.

The outcomes drive additional interventions.

The system becomes both the problem and the solution.

This is why simple explanations often fail.

People look for a root cause.

Many systems have no single root cause.

They have loops.

The behavior emerges from continuous interaction.

Understanding the Loop

The challenge becomes understanding which loops are operating.

Some loops stabilize systems.

Others destabilize them.

Some create resilience.

Others create fragility.

Some amplify positive outcomes.

Others amplify failure.

The loops themselves are neither good nor bad.

They simply exist.

The consequences depend on what they reinforce.

One reason feedback loops are difficult to see is that they often unfold slowly.

The cause and effect may be separated by:

  • weeks
  • months
  • years

People observe the outcome without recognizing the cycle that produced it.

As a result, interventions frequently target symptoms instead of loops.

The symptom disappears temporarily.

The loop remains.

Eventually the symptom returns.

Observers become confused.

The system is behaving exactly as the loop predicts.

The intervention never addressed the underlying mechanism.

The Operator's Perspective

The most effective operators learn to search for feedback.

Not just events.

Not just actions.

Not just decisions.

Feedback.

They ask:

  • What is reinforcing this behavior?
  • What is discouraging this behavior?
  • What happens as a result of this outcome?
  • How does the system react to itself?

These questions reveal dynamics that static analysis often misses.

A system diagram may show components.

A feedback analysis shows behavior.

And behavior is where most of the important answers reside.

Why Complex Systems Feel Alive

This is one reason complex systems often appear alive.

Not because they possess consciousness.

Because they adapt.

They react.

They evolve.

They learn.

Not intentionally.

Systemically.

The outputs become inputs.

The effects become causes.

The future begins influencing itself.

The output becomes an input.<br>
The effect influences the cause.<br><br>
The system begins reacting to itself.

Feedback loops are not an exception within complex systems.

They are often the mechanism that drives them.

Once you begin looking for them, you discover they are everywhere.

And once you see them, it becomes difficult to understand systems any other way.
