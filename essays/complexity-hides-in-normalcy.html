Essay 015

Complexity Hides in Normalcy

An Operational Reflection on Failure and Adaptation

Tommy Burke • 2026 • ~6 minute read

Prefer a PDF copy? Enter your email and I'll send it directly to you.

One of the most dangerous assumptions people make is believing that significant failure announces itself.

It usually does not.

Most major failures begin looking completely normal.

The signs exist.

The evidence exists.

The indicators exist.

They simply blend into the background.

This is one of the defining characteristics of complex systems.

Complex systems rarely fail all at once.

They drift.

They adapt.

They compensate.

They accumulate pressure.

For long periods of time, everything appears stable.

Then suddenly, something breaks.

Observers often describe the event as unexpected.

A surprise.

A shock.

An unforeseeable failure.

In reality, the failure was usually developing long before it became visible.

The problem was not the absence of signals.

The problem was the abundance of normalcy.

The Danger of Normal

Normalcy is powerful.

Human beings are remarkably good at adapting to conditions.

Even unhealthy conditions.

Even dangerous conditions.

Over time, unusual circumstances become familiar.

Familiar circumstances become accepted.

Accepted circumstances become invisible.

The system gradually recalibrates its definition of normal.

This phenomenon appears everywhere.

A technical debt backlog grows slowly.

No individual increase seems significant.

Years later, development slows to a crawl.

An organization accumulates small inefficiencies.

Each one appears manageable.

Eventually coordination becomes impossible.

A family experiences minor communication breakdowns.

The issues seem temporary.

The relationship deteriorates over time.

A nation accumulates economic pressures.

Each challenge appears solvable.

Eventually a crisis emerges.

The collapse feels sudden.

The causes were not.

The system was adapting the entire time.

Compensation Is Not Resilience

This is one reason experienced operators become suspicious of prolonged normalcy.

Not because normal conditions are inherently dangerous.

Because normal conditions often conceal accumulating complexity.

A lack of visible problems does not necessarily indicate the absence of problems.

Sometimes it indicates that the system is successfully compensating.

Compensation can be valuable.

Compensation can also be deceptive.

A team works overtime to keep a project on schedule.

The schedule appears healthy.

The hidden cost is exhaustion.

An administrator manually resolves recurring issues.

The service appears reliable.

The hidden cost is dependency.

An organization relies on a handful of exceptional individuals.

Performance remains strong.

The hidden cost is fragility.

The visible outcome appears stable.

The underlying system becomes increasingly vulnerable.

Observers see success.

The system experiences growing risk.

This creates a dangerous illusion.

The system appears healthy precisely because people are compensating for its weaknesses.

The compensation becomes mistaken for resilience.

In reality, the system may be becoming more fragile.

Invisible Complexity

The challenge is that complexity rarely announces itself.

Complexity accumulates quietly.

Additional processes.

Additional dependencies.

Additional exceptions.

Additional workarounds.

Additional assumptions.

Each addition appears reasonable.

Each addition appears manageable.

Eventually the interactions become impossible to fully understand.

The system becomes difficult to predict.

The risk becomes difficult to see.

Yet daily operations continue.

Normalcy persists.

This is why some of the most important indicators are often indirect.

  • Increased coordination requirements
  • Growing reliance on key individuals
  • Expanding exception lists
  • Rising operational effort
  • Decreasing tolerance for disruption

These signals often reveal more than traditional performance metrics.

The metrics may remain healthy.

The complexity may be growing.

The distinction matters.

Looking for Pressure

Many failures are not caused by a single event.

They are caused by accumulated complexity reaching a threshold.

A final event occurs.

People blame the event.

The event was merely the trigger.

The conditions already existed.

This is why effective operators spend so much time looking for pressure.

Not just failure.

Pressure.

Pressure reveals where complexity is accumulating.

Pressure reveals where compensation is occurring.

Pressure reveals where the system is struggling to maintain normalcy.

The goal is not to eliminate complexity.

Complexity is unavoidable.

The goal is to recognize it before it becomes invisible.

Because invisible complexity is often the most dangerous kind.

Normal should never be confused with healthy.

Complex systems often appear stable right up until the moment they fail.

The systems that eventually fail rarely look catastrophic beforehand.

They usually look normal.

Meetings occur.

Reports are submitted.

Projects continue.

Metrics remain acceptable.

The system appears stable.

Until it isn't.

Complexity hides in normalcy.

That is what makes it dangerous.

And that is why normal should never be confused with healthy.
