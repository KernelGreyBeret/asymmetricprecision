Essay 023

Observation Before Action

An Operational Reflection on Diagnosis and Intervention

Tommy Burke • 2026 • ~5 minute read

Prefer a PDF copy? Enter your email and I'll send it directly to you.

Human beings have a strong bias toward action.

When something breaks, we want to fix it.

When something fails, we want to intervene.

When something appears wrong, we want to correct it.

The impulse is understandable.

Action feels productive.

Observation feels passive.

Unfortunately, this instinct often creates new problems.

The Temptation to Solve Before Understanding

Many people attempt to solve problems before they understand them.

They see a symptom and immediately prescribe a solution.

A process slows down.

Add more resources.

A metric declines.

Implement new controls.

A system fails.

Replace a component.

An employee struggles.

Provide additional training.

Sometimes these interventions work.

Often they do not.

Not because the solutions are bad.

Because the diagnosis was incomplete.

Observe First. Act Second.

The most effective operators learn a different habit.

Observe first.

Act second.

This principle appears simple.

In practice, it is surprisingly difficult.

Observation requires:

  • patience
  • curiosity
  • humility
  • admitting that you may not yet understand what is happening

Action, by contrast, provides immediate psychological satisfaction.

It feels decisive.

It feels useful.

It feels like progress.

But movement and progress are not always the same thing.

A person can move very quickly in the wrong direction.

Complex Systems Punish Premature Intervention

Complex systems make this especially dangerous.

Organizations.

Networks.

Teams.

Markets.

Governments.

Families.

None of these systems operate through simple cause-and-effect relationships.

Outcomes emerge from:

  • interactions
  • feedback loops
  • dependencies
  • constraints
  • incentives
  • history

When observers focus only on the visible symptom, they often miss the forces creating it.

A security team discovers suspicious traffic and immediately blocks it.

Later they discover they interrupted a critical business process.

A leader notices declining performance and introduces new oversight.

The oversight becomes the source of the performance decline.

An organization experiences delays and adds additional approval layers.

The approvals create even more delays.

The original problem remains.

The intervention becomes a new problem.

Observation Creates Understanding

Observation helps prevent this.

Observation reveals context.

Observation identifies patterns.

Observation exposes relationships.

Observation transforms assumptions into evidence.

The goal is not inaction.

The goal is informed action.

Good operators are not slow to act.

They are deliberate about acting.

They understand that premature action often creates unnecessary complexity.

A useful question is:

What would I need to observe before I could confidently explain this outcome?

That question shifts attention away from solutions and toward understanding.

Understanding changes everything.

The moment a system becomes understandable, the range of effective actions narrows dramatically.

Many potential solutions disappear.

A few become obvious.

Calm Is Not Passivity

This is one reason experienced operators often appear calm during chaos.

They are not ignoring the problem.

They are observing it.

While others rush to intervene, they are:

  • collecting information
  • building context
  • testing assumptions
  • looking for relationships

To an outside observer, this can appear passive.

In reality, it is preparation.

The observation phase is often where the most important work occurs.

A poor diagnosis followed by perfect execution still produces poor results.

A strong diagnosis followed by competent execution often succeeds.

The difference lies in understanding.

Observation is how understanding begins.

Beyond Technical Systems

The principle extends beyond technical systems.

It applies to:

  • people
  • relationships
  • leadership
  • organizations
  • conflict

Many disagreements become worse because people attempt to solve them before understanding them.

Many failures persist because people attempt to fix symptoms instead of causes.

Many opportunities are missed because people act before they observe.

Action has tremendous value.

But action should follow understanding.

Not replace it.

Final Thought

The world often rewards decisiveness.

Reality rewards accuracy.

The most effective operators recognize the difference.

They know that every problem presents itself twice.

First as something to observe.

Then as something to solve.

Those who reverse the order often spend their time fighting symptoms.

Those who preserve the order are more likely to discover causes.

First observe.<br>
Then understand.<br>
Then act.

Observation before action.

Not because action is unimportant.

Because understanding is.
