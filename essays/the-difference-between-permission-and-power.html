Essay 009

The Difference Between Permission and Power

An Operational Reflection on Authority and Capability

Tommy Burke • 2026 • ~5 minute read

Prefer a PDF copy? Enter your email and I'll send it directly to you.

Permission and Power

People often confuse permission with power.

The confusion is understandable.

The two concepts frequently overlap.

They are not the same thing.

Permission is authorization.

Power is influence.

A person may possess one without possessing the other.

Understanding the difference explains much of what happens inside organizations.

Permission answers:

"What are you allowed to do?"

Power answers:

"What can you actually cause to happen?"

These questions are related.

They are not identical.

A manager may possess formal authority to make a decision.

Yet lack the influence necessary to implement it.

The manager has permission.

Someone else possesses the power.

A technical expert may have no formal authority at all.

No title.

No direct reports.

No budget.

Yet everyone seeks their opinion before acting.

The expert lacks permission.

The expert possesses power.

The distinction appears everywhere.

Organizational charts describe permission.

Actual behavior often reveals power.

The chart identifies reporting relationships.

The system reveals influence relationships.

The two networks frequently differ.

This surprises people.

It should not.

Permission is assigned.

Power is earned, accumulated, inherited, delegated, or granted by others.

Permission originates from structure.

Power originates from reality.

The difference becomes obvious during conflict.

When priorities align, permission and power often appear identical.

When priorities conflict, the distinction emerges.

A leader issues a directive.

The organization quietly ignores it.

Permission existed.

Power did not.

An individual without formal authority raises a concern.

Plans change immediately.

Power existed.

Permission did not.

The system reveals itself.

The Visible System and the Invisible System

Many organizations spend enormous effort managing permission.

Far fewer understand power.

This creates blind spots.

Leaders assume authority because they possess authority.

Influential individuals shape outcomes without appearing on organizational charts.

Critical decisions emerge through relationships rather than formal processes.

The organization behaves differently than its structure suggests.

The map and the territory diverge.

The consequences can be significant.

Formal governance may exist.

Informal influence may dominate.

Official priorities may be published.

Actual priorities may be determined elsewhere.

The organization becomes difficult to understand unless both systems are visible.

The visible system governs permission.

The invisible system governs power.

Permission describes the structure.

Power describes the behavior.

Both influence outcomes.

But they are not the same thing.

Why the Difference Matters

This principle extends beyond organizations.

Governments possess legal authority.

Citizens possess economic influence.

Institutions possess formal control.

Public opinion shapes behavior.

Parents possess authority.

Children possess leverage.

Every system contains both permission and power.

The two interact continuously.

The most effective leaders understand this.

They do not assume permission automatically creates power.

Nor do they assume power always requires permission.

Instead, they seek alignment between the two.

When authority and influence support one another, systems become more coherent.

When they diverge, friction emerges.

Politics emerge.

Workarounds emerge.

Informal structures emerge.

The organization develops a second operating model beneath the official one.

Most people learn to navigate this intuitively.

They know who signs the document.

They also know who actually matters.

The names are not always the same.

This reality makes some people uncomfortable.

It should not.

Power itself is not inherently good or bad.

It is simply a feature of complex systems.

People trust certain individuals.

Expertise attracts influence.

Relationships create leverage.

Reputation creates authority independent of title.

The system adapts accordingly.

Ignoring these dynamics does not eliminate them.

It merely makes them harder to understand.

One of the most useful questions an operator can ask is:

"Who can stop this?"

A second question is equally valuable:

"Who can make this happen?"

The answers often reveal more about the system than any organizational chart ever could.

Because permission describes the structure.

Power describes the behavior.

Both matter.

Both influence outcomes.

But they are not the same thing.

Permission grants authority.

Power creates consequences.

The strongest leaders understand both.

The wisest systems account for both.

And the most effective operators learn very quickly which one they are dealing with.
