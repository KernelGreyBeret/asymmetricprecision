Essay 006

Maps Are Not Territory

An Operational Reflection on Models and Reality

Tommy Burke • 2026 • ~5 minute read

Prefer a PDF copy? Enter your email and I'll send it directly to you.

A map can guide movement.
A map cannot replace observation.
The territory always gets the final vote.

One of the most dangerous mistakes people make is confusing a model with reality.

The mistake is so common that most people do not even realize they are making it.

A map is not the terrain.

A blueprint is not the building.

A policy is not the organization.

A dashboard is not the system.

An architecture diagram is not the architecture.

Yet people behave as though they are.

Representations Are Not Reality

This confusion creates problems everywhere.

A leader studies a performance report and believes they understand the organization.

An analyst reviews intelligence and believes they understand the battlefield.

An architect reviews diagrams and believes they understand the environment.

A security assessor reviews documentation and believes they understand security.

The documents are real.

The reports are real.

The diagrams are real.

But they are representations.

Not reality itself.

Representations are useful because reality is too complicated to hold entirely in our minds.

Maps simplify.

Models simplify.

Policies simplify.

Architectures simplify.

Without simplification, understanding would be impossible.

The problem begins when we forget that simplification occurred.

Every map leaves something out.

Every model makes assumptions.

Every diagram ignores details.

Every policy abstracts reality.

The moment we forget this, we begin making decisions based on the representation instead of the environment it was meant to describe.

When the Map and Territory Diverge

This is where organizations get into trouble.

A network diagram shows connectivity.

The actual network behaves differently.

An inventory says every asset is accounted for.

The environment contains hundreds of unmanaged systems.

A policy defines how work should occur.

The workforce develops entirely different practices to accomplish the mission.

An organizational chart describes authority.

Influence flows through completely different channels.

The map and the territory slowly diverge.

At first the difference is small.

Over time it grows.

Eventually people begin managing the map instead of the terrain.

Metrics improve.

Reports look healthy.

Dashboards show success.

Reality continues to deteriorate.

The Representation Becomes the Objective

This phenomenon appears everywhere.

Students learn formulas without understanding principles.

Organizations track metrics without understanding performance.

Governments satisfy requirements without achieving objectives.

Security teams implement controls without reducing risk.

The representation becomes the objective.

The reality becomes secondary.

The most effective operators learn to distrust perfect maps.

Not because maps are useless.

Because maps are incomplete.

Whenever a model appears to explain everything, it is usually hiding something important.

Reality is messy.

Reality contains contradictions.

Reality contains exceptions.

Reality contains human beings.

No model captures all of that.

Models Are Tools

Good operators use maps as tools.
Poor operators mistake maps for truth.
The distinction matters.

A map can guide movement.

A map cannot replace observation.

A model can support decisions.

A model cannot eliminate uncertainty.

An architecture can describe a system.

An architecture cannot become the system.

The territory always gets the final vote.

Reality does not care what the map says.

Reality only cares what exists.

Final Thought

The further a representation drifts from reality, the more dangerous it becomes.

Eventually people stop solving real problems and begin solving problems that exist only on paper.

The reports improve.

The metrics improve.

The diagrams improve.

Nothing else does.

Maps are valuable.

Models are valuable.

Policies are valuable.

Architectures are valuable.

But they are all merely representations.

The moment we forget that distinction, we begin navigating the map instead of the terrain.

And the terrain always wins.
